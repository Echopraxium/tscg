Nuclear Reactor Typology

Nine-pole classification demonstrating enneagram-like multipolar structure. Each reactor type represents a distinct philosophical approach to nuclear energy generation, trading off between safety, performance, cost, and sustainability.

Overview

This poclet validates the Processor GenericConcept in a complex industrial engineering domain, demonstrating dual bicephalous architecture where Territory captures empirical fission dynamics and Map represents safety models and regulatory standards.

Key Features:

  • N=9 Polarity (second validation after Yggdrasil)
  • Complete ASFID coverage (mean: 0.90)

PWR — Pressurized Water Reactor

Philosophy: Proven, safe, standardized design. Most common reactor type worldwide (70% of global fleet).

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: Light Water (H₂O)
  • Coolant: Pressurized Light Water (155 bar, 325°C)
  • Spectrum: Thermal neutrons
  • Fuel: 3-5% enriched U-235
  • Typical Power: 1000-1650 MWe
  • Efficiency: 33-37%

Advantages: Compact core, high power density, proven reliability. Disadvantages: Requires enriched fuel, high pressure vessel, cannot refuel online.

BWR — Boiling Water Reactor

Philosophy: Simplicity through fewer components. Water boils directly in reactor core.

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: Light Water
  • Coolant: Boiling Light Water
  • Spectrum: Thermal neutrons
  • Fuel: 2.5-3.5% enriched U-235
  • Typical Power: 800-1400 MWe
  • Efficiency: 33-34%

Advantages: Simpler design, no steam generators. Disadvantages: Radioactivity throughout steam system, lower thermal efficiency.

FNR — Fast Neutron Reactor

Philosophy: Breeding and sustainability. Creates more fuel than it consumes.

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: None (fast neutrons)
  • Coolant: Liquid sodium
  • Spectrum: Fast neutrons (no moderation)
  • Fuel: Plutonium + depleted uranium
  • Typical Power: 250-1500 MWe
  • Breeding Ratio: 1.2-1.4

Advantages: Plutonium breeding, 60× better uranium utilization. Disadvantages: Sodium-water reaction risk, complex fuel handling, proliferation concerns.

PHWR — Pressurized Heavy Water Reactor

Philosophy: Energy independence through natural uranium fuel (CANDU design).

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: Heavy Water (D₂O)
  • Coolant: Pressurized Heavy Water
  • Spectrum: Thermal neutrons
  • Fuel: Natural uranium (no enrichment)
  • Typical Power: 600-900 MWe
  • Efficiency: 30-32%

Advantages: No enrichment needed, online refueling, fuel flexibility. Disadvantages: Large heavy water inventory, tritium production, lower efficiency.

RBMK — Soviet Graphite-Moderated Reactor

Philosophy: Historical Soviet design. Chernobyl legacy teaches passive safety importance.

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: Graphite blocks
  • Coolant: Boiling Light Water
  • Spectrum: Thermal neutrons
  • Fuel: 2% enriched U-235
  • Typical Power: 1000 MWe
  • Status: Obsolete (being decommissioned)

Advantages: Online refueling, simple fuel design. Disadvantages: Positive void coefficient, no containment, Chernobyl accident legacy. All units now shutdown or decommissioning.

HTR — High Temperature Reactor

Philosophy: Inherent safety through physics, not engineered systems. Passive meltdown protection.

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: Graphite
  • Coolant: Helium gas (70-90 bar)
  • Spectrum: Thermal neutrons
  • Fuel: TRISO particles in graphite pebbles/prisms
  • Temperature: 750-950°C outlet
  • Efficiency: 40-50% (high-temp Brayton cycle)

Advantages: Meltdown impossible, high efficiency, hydrogen production capable. Disadvantages: Limited commercial deployment, graphite dust, high-temp materials challenge.

SMR — Small Modular Reactor

Philosophy: Modularity and flexibility. Factory-built, transportable units.

Characteristics:

  • Technology: Various (PWR, BWR, liquid metal, molten salt variants)
  • Typical Power: 10-300 MWe per module
  • Size: Factory-built, road/rail transportable
  • Safety: Enhanced passive safety features
  • Deployment: Modular scaling (1-12 units per site)

Advantages: Lower capital cost, flexible siting, passive safety, faster construction. Disadvantages: Higher cost per MWe, regulatory approval pending, limited operating experience.

MSR — Molten Salt Reactor

Philosophy: Liquid fuel revolution. Online reprocessing, waste transmutation.

Characteristics:

  • Fuel: Dissolved in molten fluoride/chloride salts (liquid)
  • Coolant: Same molten salt (fuel = coolant)
  • Spectrum: Thermal or Fast (depending on design)
  • Temperature: 600-700°C
  • Unique: Continuous fuel processing, waste burning capability

Advantages: Atmospheric pressure, online fission product removal, thorium cycle possible, excellent safety. Disadvantages: Corrosive salts, tritium containment, unproven at commercial scale.

AGR — Advanced Gas-Cooled Reactor

Philosophy: High efficiency through CO₂ cooling. Proven UK design.

Characteristics:

  • Moderator: Graphite
  • Coolant: Carbon dioxide gas (40 bar, 650°C)
  • Spectrum: Thermal neutrons
  • Fuel: 2.5-3.5% enriched uranium oxide
  • Typical Power: 600-680 MWe
  • Efficiency: 41-42% (highest among Gen II)

Advantages: Very high efficiency, online refueling, proven reliability (40+ years). Disadvantages: Large prestressed concrete vessel, UK-only technology, all units decommissioning by 2030.

Transformation Pathways

The enneagram structure reveals philosophical relationships between reactor types, visualized by colored connection arcs:

🟢 Evolution (Green)

PWR → SMR: Miniaturization and passive safety evolution. Small Modular Reactors inherit PWR principles but add modularity and enhanced passive safety features.

🟡 Trade-off (Yellow)

PWR ↔ HTR: Safety philosophy trade-off between active engineered systems (PWR) and inherent physics-based safety (HTR).

FNR ↔ PHWR: Fuel strategy trade-off between plutonium breeding (FNR) and natural uranium independence (PHWR/CANDU).

🟣 Shared Vision (Purple)

FNR ↔ MSR: Both future-oriented advanced technologies sharing the vision of long-term sustainability — FNR through breeding, MSR through waste transmutation.

🔵 Historical Lineage (Cyan)

RBMK → HTR: From graphite-moderated danger (Chernobyl) to graphite-based inherent safety. HTR learned from RBMK's flaws to create passively safe design.

🟠 Safety Lesson (Orange)

RBMK → PWR: Chernobyl accident reinforced PWR safety standards worldwide, validating containment building and negative feedback coefficient importance.

🔷 Simplification (Blue)

BWR → SMR: Natural circulation principles from BWR inform SMR passive cooling designs.

AGR → HTR: Decades of graphite + gas cooling operational experience (AGR) inform Generation IV HTR development.
