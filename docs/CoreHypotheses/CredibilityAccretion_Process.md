## 1. The Problem: Is the Protocol Scientific?

The Multisubjective Score Evaluation Protocol produces scalar scores (e.g.
`A: 0.8`) through expert judgment rather than physical measurement. This
raises an immediate and legitimate challenge:

> *"A protocol based on subjective evaluation is not scientific. It could be
> compared to fortune-telling or irrational belief. And if it is not credible
> from the start, how can new cooks ever build the consensus that would make
> it credible?"*

This document addresses that challenge from four distinct observer perspectives
(cf. `m2:Observer = I × A` in M2 GenericConcepts), then identifies the
underlying mechanism — the **Credibility Accretion Spiral** — as a TSCG
Attractor in its own right.

---

## 2. The "Favourite Films" Objection

A natural analogy: *"My top 10 favourite films"* is a list that is subjective
and revisable — just like TSCG scores. Is there a real difference?

| Property | Favourite films | TSCG M0 scores |
|---|---|---|
| Criterion | Hedonic ("I enjoy this") | Structural ("this system expresses type A per explicit criteria") |
| Anchor | Internal, non-transferable state | Shared canonical poclet reference |
| Replicability | None | Targeted (same criteria → similar results) |
| Falsifiability | None | Declarable (ICC measurable as N cooks grow) |
| Explicit provisionality | No | Yes — `defeasibilityStatus` is a first-class property |

The critical distinction is **structural anchorage**: TSCG scores are not
expressions of preference but of structural degree-of-fit, grounded in
explicit monoidal type criteria and a shared canonical reference.

A fortune-teller never declares that their prediction is *"provisional,
defeasible, and calibrated at a single observer with ICC not yet computed."*
TSCG does — and that declaration is what separates structured epistemology
from arbitrary assertion.

## 3. The Bootstrap Paradox

The challenge is real and must be stated honestly:

> *If the protocol is not credible from the start, how can new cooks be
> attracted to build the consensus that would make it credible?*

This is not a flaw unique to TSCG. It is the **universal bootstrap problem**
of any emerging scientific framework. Historical precedents:

| Framework | Initial reception | Bootstrap mechanism | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plate tectonics (Wegener, 1912) | Rejected for 50 years | Sonar evidence → geologists converted | Universally accepted |
| Germ theory (Semmelweis, 1847) | Rejected; author died ignored | Pasteur + independent replication | Foundation of medicine |
| Mendelian genetics (1866) | Ignored for 35 years | Rediscovered by 3 independent labs (1900) | Foundation of biology |
| Quantum mechanics (1900–1927) | Philosophically resisted | Experimental accumulation + mathematical formalism | Physics paradigm |

In each case:
- Initial credibility was **near zero**
- The underlying structure was **real**
- A small group of **early adopters** identified the structure before consensus
- Their independent convergence initiated an **accretion spiral**

The bootstrap is not resolved by waiting for consensus — it is initiated by
the **structural coherence visible to early adopters**, which then attracts
further independent verification.

## 4. Four Observer Perspectives

Following the TSCG `m2:Observer` concept (`I × A` — information collection
anchored to an attractor reference), the credibility question is best addressed
from four distinct observational stances.

### Observer 1 — The Scientific Critic

*"Subjective protocols are unreliable and non-scientific."*

**Response**: The protocol is subjective *initially*, not *by design*. The
`objectivityLevel` field explicitly tracks the evolution from `Subjective`
through `MultiExpertConsensus` to `ContextualizedBenchmark`. The protocol
is designed to be **self-upgrading** as cooks and kitchens diversify.

What makes a measurement scientific is not its initial objectivity but its
**capacity to be falsified and improved**. TSCG scores satisfy this through:
- Explicit justification fields
- Declared `defeasibilityStatus`
- ICC-measurable inter-rater convergence
- Canonical benchmark references

### Observer 2 — The Historian of Science

*"Most revolutionary frameworks were initially rejected."*

The pattern is consistent: genuine structural discoveries are initially
legible only to those with sufficient domain depth to see past the surface
subjectivity. Early adopters do not wait for consensus — they respond to
**structural coherence**.

The difference between TSCG and pseudoscience is not the presence of early
adopters but the **falsifiability architecture**: TSCG explicitly builds in
the mechanisms by which its claims can be tested, refined, and overturned.

### Observer 3 — The TSCG Architect

*"The protocol contains its own credibility growth mechanism."*

The `scoringHistory` + `convergenceScore` (ICC) mechanism is precisely the
instrument that transforms subjective initial scores into measurable
multisubjective convergence over time. It does not assert objectivity — it
**accumulates evidence of convergence**, which is the only honest path to
objectivity for qualitative structural measurements.

### Observer 4 — The Philosopher (Peirce)

> *"The opinion which is fated to be ultimately agreed to by all who
> investigate is what we mean by truth."*
> — Charles Sanders Peirce, 1878

Translated to TSCG:

> The canonical score for a poclet is the value that all cooks, using all
> kitchens, investigating the same system, are fated to converge upon —
> provisionally, defeasibly, until a better evaluation supersedes it.

Initial credibility is not required. What is required is that the protocol
be constructed so that **convergence of N independent cooks is mathematically
measurable**. ICC provides exactly that. This is what distinguishes structured
epistemology from fortune-telling: not initial certainty, but the capacity
to quantify and falsify.

## 8. Existing Theories on Credibility Accretion

The credibility accretion process described in this document is not without
precedent. Several established theoretical frameworks independently model
the same phenomenon — providing external grounding for TSCG's protocol.

### 8.1 Latour & Woolgar — The "Credibility Cycle" (1979)

The most direct precedent. In *Laboratory Life* (1979), Chapter 5 explicitly
formalises the **"cycle of credibility"** among scientists — the mechanism
by which scientific credit accumulates in a research community, exactly as
economic capital accumulates in society. Latour models scientific truth as
grounded in **credibility determined by the strength of associations**: the
more actors (human and non-human) converge on a claim, the more "real" it
becomes.

This is structurally identical to the TSCG accretion spiral: more cooks +
more kitchens → stronger convergence → more credible canonical scores.

**Key distinction**: Latour describes the phenomenon sociologically. TSCG
adds an *operational measurement instrument* (ICC via `scoringHistory`) that
Latour's framework lacks.

### 8.2 Actor-Network Theory — ANT (Latour, Callon, Law, 1986+)

ANT extends the credibility cycle to include non-human actors. A scientific
fact becomes established only when a sufficient **network** of actors —
human experts, instruments, publications, institutions, *and artefacts* —
support it.

In TSCG terms: canonical poclets themselves, SHACL grammar files, and
`.jsonld` ontologies are **non-human actors** in the credibility network,
participating alongside human cooks. The SHACL grammar that can *reject*
a poclet is a non-human actor enforcing structural coherence.

### 8.3 Kuhn — Paradigm Shifts and Early Adopters (1962)

Kuhn identifies that paradigm-shifting frameworks are typically initiated
by those "either very young or very new to the field" — least committed
to prior rules, most likely to see that the existing framework has
structural limits. Early adopters do not wait for consensus: they respond
to **structural coherence visible before the consensus exists**.

This is the resolution to the bootstrap paradox: initial credibility is
not required. Structural coherence, legible to a small number of early
adopters, initiates the accretion spiral.

### 8.4 The Credibility Thesis — Institutional Economics

The *Credibility Thesis* (heterodox institutional economics) posits that
institutions emerge from intentional building but never in their originally
intended form — their persistence is explained by **the function they
serve** rather than their theoretical form. Credibility is endogenous and
spontaneously ordered.

Applied to TSCG: the protocol's credibility will ultimately be established
by the structural utility it provides to cooks (helping them model systems
more rigorously), not by the theoretical elegance of its design.

### 8.5 Synthesis: Where TSCG Stands

| Theory | What it contributes | What TSCG adds |
|---|---|---|
| Latour cycle of credibility | Names the phenomenon, models it as capital accumulation | Operational ICC measurement |
| ANT | Non-human actors in credibility networks | SHACL grammar as structural enforcer |
| Kuhn paradigm shifts | Early adopters + structural coherence as bootstrap | Multi-kitchen diversity as bootstrap mechanism |
| Credibility Thesis | Functional utility drives persistence | Explicit defeasibility architecture |

TSCG does not reinvent these theories — it **instantiates** them in a
specific transdisciplinary ontological context, adds a measurable protocol
(scoringHistory + ICC), and makes the process **formally trackable**.

## 9. Intellectual Honesty: Against "Intellectual Self-Gratification"

The most devastating critique of any abstract transdisciplinary framework
is that it is *"intellectually self-gratifying"* — a system that can dress
any phenomenon in its vocabulary and produce scores that appear to validate
its own theory. This is a legitimate risk for TSCG, given:

- A proprietary vocabulary (poclet, ASFID, REVOI, δ₁...)
- Abstract monoidal types difficult to "touch"
- Scores initially evaluated by the framework's creator
- A framework applicable to *anything* — which is itself suspicious

### The Structural Safeguards

The mechanisms documented across this document and
`MultisubjectiveScoreEvaluationProtocol.md` constitute the architectural
response to this risk:

| Critique | Safeguard |
|---|---|
| "Self-proving" | `objectivityLevel` declared + `scoringHistory` multi-cooks |
| "Arbitrary scores" | ICC measurable + canonical benchmark per M1 domain |
| "Applicable to everything therefore nothing" | SHACL grammar — formal constraints that can **reject** an instance |
| "Not falsifiable" | `defeasibilityStatus` = provisional by design |
| "Opaque vocabulary" | Anchored in Latour, Peirce, Lambek calculus — established traditions |

The critical point: TSCG scores can **fail**. A poclet can be rejected by
SHACL. A score can be contradicted by an independent cook. δ₁ can reveal
an incoherence between Eagle Eye and Sphinx Eye. These possible failure
modes are precisely what distinguishes a structured epistemological
framework from a self-referential closed system.
