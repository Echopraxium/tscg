## 1. System Overview

**Counterpoint** is a compositional system in which N independent melodic voices (*agents*) coexist simultaneously in time, each pursuing its own melodic trajectory while respecting a shared set of normative constraints — the **Counterpoint Rules**. The system produces **harmonic coherence as an emergent property** of local rule compliance: no single voice "knows" the global harmony; it arises from pairwise interactions governed by the rule set.

Counterpoint is TSCG's first poclet in the **aesthetic domain**. ## 2. Architecture — 4 Poles

The lifecycle of a fugal composition follows four discrete functional phases:

| Pole | Name | Voice Density | Tension Level |
|------|------|--------------|---------------|
| `pole_1_exposition` | **Exposition** | Progressive 1→N | Low |
| `pole_2_development` | **Development** (Episodes + Middle Entries) | Full N, variable | Medium |
| `pole_3_stretto` | **Stretto** | Full N, maximum overlap | High |
| `pole_4_resolution` | **Resolution** (Coda) | Full N, converging | Decreasing → zero |

### Pole 1 — Exposition
System initialization. All N voices enter successively, each announcing the **Subject** (original) or **Answer** (transposed to the fifth). Voice 1 states the Subject alone; Voice 2 enters with the Answer while Voice 1 begins the **Countersubject** — the first instance of genuine autonomous counter-melody. The process continues until all voices have entered and the full polyphonic texture is established.

### Pole 2 — Development
System exploration. **Episodes** — passages without complete Subject presentation — modulate through related keys (dominant, relative major/minor, etc.) using motivic fragments derived from Subject and Countersubject. **Middle entries** re-present the Subject in new keys, expanding the tonal narrative. The Development is the fugue's "wandering phase": the system explores harmonic space before returning to the tonic attractor.

### Pole 3 — Stretto
Structural climax. Subject entries in successive voices **overlap** — Voice B begins the Subject before Voice A has completed it. The propagation cycle is compressed to its minimum temporal interval. Polyphonic density reaches its maximum. Not all subjects permit stretto (the Subject must be harmonically self-compatible when overlapped) — so the presence of stretto is itself evidence of compositional mastery.

### Pole 4 — Resolution (Coda)
System closure. Tonic key reestablished decisively. Final Subject entry (often over a **pedal point** — sustained tonic bass note). Authentic cadence (V⁷ → I) releases all accumulated harmonic tension. In Baroque practice, minor-mode fugues often end on a **Picardy third** (tonic major chord) — a final gesture of luminous resolution.

## 6. Cross-Stylistic Validation

The same ASFID structure is identifiable across radically different instantiations of counterpoint:

### Canon — Frère Jacques (folk round)
The simplest possible counterpoint: one melody, 4 voices, pure time offset (2-measure delay). All ASFID dimensions present despite minimal complexity. Pedagogically indispensable: zero prior musical knowledge required. Every child in the Western tradition has *lived* this system.

```
Voice 1: [Frère Jacques...] 
Voice 2:       [Frère Jacques...]  ← enters 2 measures later, exact imitation
Voice 3:             [Frère Jacques...]
Voice 4:                   [Frère Jacques...]
```

### BWV 578 — 'Little' Fugue in G minor (J.S. Bach) ← **Simulation Primary Reference**
4 voices, G minor, clear and pedagogically transparent structure. The Subject (descending G minor conjunct line, 4 measures) is immediately recognizable. All 4 structural poles are cleanly identifiable. Bach's most accessible complete fugue — standard teaching example and the primary reference for the interactive simulation.

### BWV 565 — Toccata and Fugue in D minor (J.S. Bach)
The most iconic fugue in the repertoire. The preceding Toccata pre-activates the dynamic dimension (ASFID D) before the Fugue proper. The D minor Subject (falling diminished seventh + stepwise run) is arguably the most recognizable subject in fugal history. Demonstrates the counterpoint system at full expressive power.

### Miles Davis Quintet — Modal Jazz Counterpoint (Herbie Hancock, Wayne Shorter, Ron Carter, Tony Williams)
Counterpoint liberated from written score. The second Miles Davis Great Quintet (1964–68) exemplifies counterpoint as real-time collective negotiation. Each performer improvises independently while sharing the harmonic grid. The ASFID structure is identical to the Baroque fugue; only the medium of the Rules changes: written notation → internalized stylistic vocabulary. The Subject is not pre-composed but emerges live from collective negotiation.
