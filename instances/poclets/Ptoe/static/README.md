## Element Families

The periodic table organizes elements into distinct families based on their electron configurations and chemical properties:

### Alkali Metals
Highly reactive metals in Group 1 with one valence electron. They form +1 cations and react vigorously with water, producing hydrogen gas and metal hydroxides. Examples: Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr.

### Alkaline Earth Metals
Reactive metals in Group 2 with two valence electrons. They form +2 cations and are less reactive than alkali metals. Their compounds are important in biological systems and construction materials. Examples: Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra.

### Transition Metals
Metals in Groups 3-12 with variable oxidation states. They have partially filled d-orbitals which enable colored compounds and catalysis. Examples: Fe, Cu, Zn, Ag, Au, Pt.

### Post-Transition Metals
Metals in Groups 13-15 that are softer and have lower melting points than transition metals. Examples: Al, Ga, In, Sn, Tl, Pb, Bi.

### Metalloids
Elements with properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals. They are semiconductors, crucial for modern electronics. Examples: B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te.

### Nonmetals
Elements that gain electrons in reactions to form anions. They form covalent bonds and are essential for life. Examples: C, N, O, P, S, Se.

### Halogens
Highly reactive nonmetals in Group 17 with seven valence electrons. They form -1 anions and exist as diatomic molecules. Examples: F, Cl, Br, I, At.

### Noble Gases
Inert gases in Group 18 with full valence shells. They rarely form compounds due to their exceptional stability. Examples: He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn.

### Lanthanides
Rare earth metals in the f-block (atomic numbers 57-71). They have similar chemical properties and are used in magnets, phosphors, and electronics. Examples: Ce, Nd, Eu, Gd, Tb.

### Actinides
Radioactive elements in the f-block (atomic numbers 89-103). Many are synthetic and unstable. They include nuclear fuels. Examples: Th, U, Np, Pu, Am, Cm.

## Quantum Constraints

The periodic table's structure emerges from quantum mechanical principles:
- **Shell Structure**: Electrons occupy discrete energy levels
- **Subshell Filling**: s, p, d, f orbitals fill according to Aufbau principle
- **Pauli Exclusion**: Maximum 2 electrons per orbital
- **Hund's Rule**: Orbitals within a subshell filled singly before pairing
